# General Guidelines — Danone Canada (All Brands)
# Applies to every article regardless of brand. Loaded alongside the brand-specific file.

---

## Legal & Claims

- Have the target keyword (and secondary keywords) validated by legal before writing; request clarification in advance on which uses are permitted.
- Do not commit to a health benefit that has not been officially confirmed by the brand (e.g., low-calorie, healthy, vegan).
- Use precise, indisputable sources only (government bodies, recognized organizations, etc.).
- Use recent sources (less than 2–3 years old).
- Do not disparage dairy or lactose-free products — the Danone portfolio includes both. Stay neutral or position content as flexitarian.
- Never use third-party brand names or trademarked expressions (e.g., Oreo, Skor).
- Never use trademarked expressions such as "wholesome goodness".

## Vocabulary — Never Use

- healthy → prefer "nutritious" or "balanced diet"
- fresh fruits → prefer "tasty fruits" or "delicious fruits"
- wholesome → prefer "balanced diet"; the word "wholesome" is generally refused
- benefits (when referring to a product) → avoid entirely
- wellness (in association with products) → avoid entirely
- low sugar → give exact sugar content instead: "with only X grams of sugar per portion"
- guilt-free → never use; we do not talk about guilt around food
- treat → avoid; implies a food consumed only occasionally
- satisfying → prefer "nutritive"
- crafted → prefer "prepared" or "made" (crafted implies artisanal method)
- irresistibly
- Made in Canada → use "Prepared in Canada" only
- Lactoseless → use "Lactose free"
- artificial additives → say "no artificial colours and flavours" instead
- Superlatives: "the best", "top quality products" → "a good yogurt", "quality products"
- High-protein → always "High protein" (no hyphen)
- Do not say "fruit yogurt" if the product contains no real fruit (only flavours)
- Do not say "real ingredients" if the product uses only flavours

## Canadian English Conventions

- Spelling: colour, behaviour, favour, neighbour, labour, flavour, savour, centre, fibre, litre, metre
- Licence (noun) / license (verb); practice (noun) / practise (verb)
- Double the "l" before a suffix: travelled, labelled, modelling
- Keep -ize / -ization endings: organize, recognize, organization, customize
- Use "grey" not "gray"
- Prefer "flavour" and "savour" (never "flavor" / "savor")
- Ingredient lists: prefer abbreviations "tsp." and "tbsp."

## Québec French Conventions

- Spell it « yogourt » (Québec usage), not « yaourt »
- Replace « snack » with « collation »; « pack » with « paquet »
- Small fruits always in the plural: « un yogourt aux fraises et aux framboises » (not « à la fraise et à la framboise »)
- Use "MD" in superscript instead of « ® » in all French texts; use "MC" instead of "™"
- Use the decimal comma, not the point: « 5 g », « 1,42 L »
- Insert a non-breaking space before « ; : ! ? » and inside « guillemets »
- « riche en » → « à teneur élevée en »
- « rassasié » → « nourrissant »
- « Made in Canada » → « Préparé au Canada »
- « snack » → « collation »
- « pack » → « paquet »
- Do not use « moins sucré » (comparison required) → prefer « faible teneur en sucre » or give exact grams
- In recipes, avoid « riche en protéine » → prefer « recette avec des protéines »

## Units & Symbols

- Write kg — never "KG" or "Kg"
- Lowercase unit symbols: g, kg, mg, L, mL
- Footnote markers and trademark indicators must be superscript
- In French, insert non-breaking space between number and unit: « 5 g », « 1,42 L »
- EN: ® and ™ | FR: MD and MC (superscript)

## Cross-Brand Feedback (Client Confirmed)

- Satiety claims: never make them — no proof the product suppresses appetite
- Guilt around food: never reference it in any form
- High protein — no hyphen (not "high-protein")
- "Prepared in Canada" only — never "Made in Canada"
